Same task, two outcomes

Three common assignment types, and what unethical vs. ethical AI use looks like in each.

1. Essay paragraph (history)

Don't do this

Prompt AI: "Write me 300 words on the causes of the French Revolution." Copy the output directly into your essay, change two words, submit.

Risk: undisclosed AI authorship, no engagement with sources, can't answer follow-up questions in class.

Do this instead

Write your own paragraph from your notes. Ask AI: "Does this paragraph have any factual errors or weak logic?" Revise based on its feedback, in your own words.

Result: your argument, AI used as a proofreader/critic — disclose per your course policy.

2. Code assignment (intro programming)

Don't do this

Paste the assignment brief into AI, get a full working program back, submit it without reading it line by line.

Risk: you can't explain your own submission in a viva or follow-up exam question. The exam is where this catches up with you.

Do this instead

Write your own attempt first. When stuck, ask AI: "Why doesn't this loop terminate?" instead of "fix this." Type the fix yourself once you understand it.

Result: working code you actually understand, and a debugging skill you keep.

3. Exam-style revision (any subject)

Don't do this

Ask AI to "summarise the whole chapter" and read the summary once, assuming that counts as revision.

Risk: passive reading doesn't build recall. AI isn't in the exam room with you.

Do this instead

Ask AI to quiz you with questions one at a time, mark your answers, and explain what you got wrong — active recall, not passive reading.

Result: you find your gaps before the exam does.

A note on AI detectors

AI-detection tools are unreliable in both directions — they flag real human writing and miss AI writing fairly often. This course doesn't teach you to beat them, because that's the wrong target. The actual target is understanding your own work well enough that it wouldn't matter if a detector ever looked at it.
