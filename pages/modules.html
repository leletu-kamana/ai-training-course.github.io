Five modules, in order

Each module is a 5–10 minute read. Work through them in order if you're new to this — each one builds on the last. Opening a module marks it complete in your progress bar below (stored only in your browser).

Module 1

What "academic integrity" actually protects

+

Academic integrity rules aren't there to make your life harder. They exist so that a qualification — a degree, a certificate, a pass mark — actually means you can do the thing it says you can do.

When you submit AI-generated or plagiarised work as your own, you're not "getting away with" anything. You're collecting a credential that doesn't match your actual skills. That gap shows up later — in a job, an exam, or the next course that assumes you already know this.

Key idea

Plagiarism isn't just "copying text." It includes submitting AI output as your own original work without disclosure, even if you changed a few words.

Module 2

The copy-paste trap

+

It's tempting to ask an AI tool for an answer and paste it straight into your assignment. It feels efficient. It isn't — here's why:

  • You can't defend work you don't understand. If a teacher asks you to explain your own paragraph and you can't, that's a bigger problem than a low mark.
  • AI gets things wrong. Confidently. Pasted text carries those errors straight into your grade.
  • You lose the actual skill. The assignment was never really about the output — it was practice for a skill you'll need later without AI in the room.

Try this instead

Ask the AI to explain the concept to you, close the chat, and write your answer from memory. Then check your draft against the explanation.

Module 3

Disclosure: telling your institution you used AI

+

Most schools and universities now publish an AI use policy. They usually fall into one of three categories:

  • Permitted with disclosure — you can use AI but must say how, usually in a statement or footnote.
  • Permitted for specific tasks only — e.g. brainstorming or grammar checking, but not drafting content.
  • Not permitted — typically for exams, tests, or specific assessed tasks.

Always check your specific module or course guide — policies vary even within the same institution. When in doubt, ask your lecturer or teacher directly; it's a completely normal question to ask.

Key idea

"I didn't know I had to disclose it" is rarely accepted as an excuse. Check the policy before you start, not after you submit.

Module 4

Citing AI assistance properly

+

If your assignment permits AI use with disclosure, you'll typically need to cite it — similar to citing any other source. The exact format depends on your referencing style.

See the full Citation Guide for APA, Harvard, and MLA examples with real templates you can copy.

Key idea

A citation doesn't excuse copy-pasting. You still need to engage with and understand what the AI produced — citing it just makes your process transparent.

Module 5

Using AI as a tutor, not a ghostwriter

+

Used well, AI is one of the best study tools available. Some genuinely useful patterns:

  • Concept explainer: "Explain photosynthesis like I'm in Grade 10, then quiz me on it."
  • Draft critic: Write your own draft first, then ask AI to point out weak arguments or unclear sentences — not to rewrite them for you.
  • Study partner: "Ask me five questions about this chapter, one at a time, and tell me if I'm wrong."
  • Debugging help (code): Ask AI to explain *why* code is broken before asking for a fix, so you learn the pattern.
